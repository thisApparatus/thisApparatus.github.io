This short exercise uses found objects transformed into a loudspeaker-based interface, applying biosemiotics principles—such as Uexküll’s Umwelt theory and the semiosphere—as well as Reybrouck’s explanation of the enactive approach to cognition following the theoretical framework established by Kass in the paper 𝘛𝘦𝘤𝘩𝘯𝘰𝘭𝘰𝘨𝘺 𝘢𝘯𝘥 𝘐𝘯𝘵𝘦𝘳𝘴𝘱𝘦𝘤𝘪𝘦𝘴 𝘔𝘶𝘴𝘪𝘤𝘢𝘭 𝘗𝘳𝘢𝘤𝘵𝘪𝘤𝘦 (2024). The result is an experimental model of individual-environment interaction, focusing on the notion of space. The performer interacts with various elements—room and contact microphones, a digital audio workstation and the speaker array—treating them as synthetic extensions of their sensory mechanisms. The speaker array responds to environmental sounds and manually triggered audio samples, activating vibrations in the drivers that are transmitted back into the space and the surface of the materials to generate new responses. Feedback loops, dynamically manipulated with digital processing and effects, embody the conceptual processing of sensory experience, continuously reinterpreting the unfolding interaction. This process fosters a dialogue between the environment and the performer, amplifying elements of their perceptual relationship. It leads to a site-specific experience driven by communication between individual and space.

Excerpts of a 90-minute exercise, capturing two room microphones and two contact microphones attached to different materials of the speaker array (processed in real time via Ableton Live), as well as an unprocessed recording of the space.
